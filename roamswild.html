The Problem

Too much data, not enough signal.

Existing tools surface what's popular — not what's actually good. When you're on the road, the problem isn't a lack of options. It's evaluating which ones are worth your time.

Optimized for popularity

Crowdsourced lists pile around the same overcrowded spots — and miss the quieter, better ones.

Fragmented across tools

Finding good campsites, scenic routes, and ideal lighting means juggling four or five apps.

"Good on paper, bad in reality"

No way to verify quality before you arrive. Decision fatigue from juggling conflicting info.

The Insight
The best spots aren't the most reviewed — they're the most contextually relevant.
My Role

Reframe the category.

Key Decisions

Quality over quantity. Always.

The biggest decisions weren't about features — they were about restraint. Saying "no" to scope kept the experience focused and the system trustworthy.

Decision 01

Quality over quantity

Surface the few options that actually match real conditions and intent — not every result.

Decision 02

Exploration, not search

Designed for browsing and serendipity. Less filtering, more guided discovery.

Decision 03

Limited scope, deep value

A few high-value experiences beat a feature-rich product. Saying no kept the bar high.

Under the Hood

Real signals. Open data. A custom scoring engine.

RoamsWild combines public APIs, terrain math, and a PostGIS database to turn raw geographic data into recommendations that explain themselves.

External APIs
Open-Meteo Tomorrow.io OpenStreetMap Recreation.gov RIDB Google Maps DEM terrain
Scoring Algorithms
DAGS Distant Atmospheric Glow Score
Rates viewpoints for layered terrain glow at sunrise/sunset using depth, openness, rim elevation, and sun-angle clearance.
VAS Visual Anchor Score
Detects salient terrain features — ridgelines, spires, mesas — inside the camera view cone using curvature and slope-break analysis.
LAA Light at Anchor
Verifies whether anchor features actually receive direct sunlight via ray-marched shadow checks against the DEM.
The Schema

A geospatial database, built for decisions.

Designing the data was half the work. RoamsWild stores boundaries, road networks, and derived spots as native PostGIS geometry — so the scoring engine can reason about real-world geography, not just rows of text.

Why this schema

Native geometry, not strings

POINT, LINESTRING, and POLYGON columns with GIST indexes — spatial queries instead of lat/lng filtering.

Layered by purpose

User data, geospatial layers, scoring outputs, and reference data live in distinct domains so each can evolve independently.

Auth-aware by default

Row-level security via Supabase Auth means user-owned data is isolated without writing custom permission logic.

Pre-computed where it matters

Region metrics and derived spots are scored offline so the live experience stays fast — no scoring at request time.

What I Learned

Designing systems is different from designing screens.

This project pushed me to treat data, scoring logic, and human judgment as core design materials — not just UI surfaces.

Designing with data, not just for it

The most important decisions were about what signals matter and how to weight them — not what the screen looked like.

Constraint is the whole game in 0→1

Deciding what NOT to build is the hardest and most valuable work. AI makes it easier than ever to ship junk fast.

AI as a creative + technical partner

Used well, AI extends what one designer can ship — but it amplifies whatever direction you give it. Direction matters more than ever.
